Accessability Software for Music

For my senior major design experience project I am taking "Accessible Software Systems Design" with Dr. David Chesney. I am working on a team with 3 other senior students to design a software for our client: Brad Ebenhoeh. In 2005 Brad had a cerebral hemorrhage after which he spent 7 months in the hospital and 10 years in rehab. As a result Brad now uses a motorized wheelchair, is paralyzed on the right-hand side of his body and left-hand side of his face, has Ataxia in the left-hand side of his body, and has visual difficulties as well. One thing Brad is very passionate about is music and playing the paino. The goal of our software is to add functionality to the Akai MPK Mini midi keyboard by repurposing some of the drum pads and incorperating a foot pedal so that it is much easier for Brad to execute certain actions, such as playing chords or switching octaves while only using one hand. Our software will be written in C++ using the JUCE application and API.

  • Organization: University of Michigan CSE Senior Design Project
  • Date: January 2018 - April 2018
  • Client: Brad Ebenhoeh

Autonomous Microrobotics at Universidad de Navarra: Tecnun

Over the course of 2 weeks during a summer study abroad program in San Sebastián, Spain, I collaborated with two other University of Michigan students and a TecNun student to create a robot consisting of Legos, a sensor board, and Arduino. The end goal of this mini-course was to compete with other teams to win different trials that were marked with tape on white posterboard. Some of these trials included navigating around a circular course, following straight and dotted lines, and picking up a cup with a gripper. I designed the algorithms with a teammate and programmed Arduino code in C allowing the robot to change its functionality/program upon pressing a button, allowing it to perform the various mechanical tasks with code specialized for each task. More information on mini-course here.

  • Organization: Universidad de Navarra: Tecnun
  • Date: May 2016

Path Planning Research

During the 12 weeks of summer between sophomore and junior year where I was not in Spain I did paid intern research for the Univerisity of Michigan EECS department under Professor Stephane Lafortune. Professor Lafortune partnered with Dr. Richard Hill of University of Detroit Mercy. I worked closely with Dr. Hill to continue his research project regarding abstracting path planning scenarios to cut down on calculation times so that planning could be calculated on the fly much more quickly. Our tests involved different numbers of robots, numerous start and end goals, and a different number of regions that only one robot could occupy at a time. My tasks over this summer included understanding the existing code base, making improvements to the basic robot movement algorithm, analyzing scenarios where the planning was slow using traditional methods and comparing these to abstracted scenarios. My final task was to program a controller to simulate parallel composition with the ability to construct a finite-state machine from various smaller finite-state machines and run Dijkstra's algorithm on the resulting graph, and then create documentation for the improvements made and a manual for running the code. In total these changes decreased the run-time of robot path planning scenarios by around half on average.

  • Organization: University of Michigan EECS Department
  • Dates: May 2016 - August 2016
